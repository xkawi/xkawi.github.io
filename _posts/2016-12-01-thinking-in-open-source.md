My first open-source contribution was a documentation fix. One sentence. I noticed a method name in the README didn't match the actual API, spent twenty minutes convincing myself I wasn't wrong, then opened a pull request. It was merged the next morning.

That was it. That was the whole thing. But it changed how I thought about software.

## The shift in perspective

Before that PR, I treated open-source libraries as infrastructure — stable, authoritative, not mine to touch. After it, they were just *code*, maintained by people who also started somewhere, who also made mistakes, who were also grateful when someone caught something they missed.

This sounds obvious. It wasn't obvious to me at the time.

## Why it compounds

Contributing to open source isn't just about the individual PR. The value compounds in ways that are hard to see at the start:

**You read production code at scale.** Not tutorial code. Not "clean" example code. Real systems with real trade-offs, shortcuts, and historical decisions baked in. Reading that code makes you a better programmer faster than almost anything else.

**You learn to communicate in writing.** Every PR needs a description. Every issue needs a reproduction case. Open source is asynchronous and global — clear written communication is the only tool you have. I got better at writing by being forced to explain my changes to strangers.

**Your work is permanent and searchable.** A commit you made three years ago is still there. Future employers, collaborators, and users can read it. There's a kind of accountability in that which makes you more careful.

**You build a network without networking.** The people who reviewed my early PRs became colleagues, references, and eventually friends. None of that happened through a conference or a cold email.

## How to start

The hardest part isn't the code — it's finding a project where you feel like you belong. Some pointers:

1. **Start with tools you actually use.** You have context. You know what the friction points are. That context is valuable.
2. **Read the issues before writing code.** A "good first issue" label is a real signal. Maintainers put it there on purpose.
3. **Fix something small first.** A typo, a broken link, a missing test. Get one merge under your belt before attempting a feature.
4. **Ask before building.** Open an issue describing what you want to do. Maintainers will tell you if it's already in progress or won't be accepted. This saves everyone time.

## What it costs

Contributing takes time. Not everything gets merged. Some maintainers are slow to respond or critical in ways that sting. These are real costs.

But the thing I keep coming back to is that working in public has made my private work better. The habits — write a clear commit message, test the edge cases, explain the why not just the what — don't stay in the open-source repo. They come with you.

That's the compounding part.
